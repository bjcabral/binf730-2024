Substitution Matrix

Substitution matrices serve two fundamental purposes in sequence alignment: a) Quantify the likelihood of one amino acid being replaced by another during evolution and b) assign scores to amino acid matches and mismatches to guide the alignment algorithm to create an optimal alignment. Some popular substitution matrices include Point Accepted Mutation (PAM), BLOck SUbstitution Matrix (BLOSUM), Variable Time Maximum Likelihood Matrices, and many other specialized matrices. For this exercise, you can also manually enter your own score schema for match, mismatch, and gap, or use a few supported by this program, namely, BLOSUM62, 50, and 80 and PAM250 and 100.

Alignment Method

Sequence alignment methods can be categorized into global and local alignment approaches. Global Alignment: Attempts to align the entire length of two sequences, maximizing the overall similarity between them. Local Alignment: Focuses on finding regions of high similarity within sequences, without necessarily aligning them end-to-end
